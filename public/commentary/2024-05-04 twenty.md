> _“We thought of life by analogy with a journey, a pilgrimage, which had a serious purpose at the end, and the thing was to get to that end, success or whatever it is, maybe heaven after you’re dead. But we missed the point the whole way along. It was a musical thing and you were supposed to sing or to dance while the music was being played.”_
— Alan Watts
> 

I often tell people that I don’t have it in me to board a cruise ship. I'm not the best traveller anyway, and a long-winded trip just floating on a massive body of water has never really sounded like the best idea to me. It's hard to explain this behaviour to people who know me because most of them are aware of the fact that I'm a One Piece fan, and it can often feel like there's something seemingly incongruous about me not liking the ocean but raving about how cool the Straw Hat Pirates are for travelling right across it.

This pair of contradictory opinions has stayed with me for a while, and so far, the closest explanation I’ve provided as a means of justifying this rift is that *adventure always feels a lot safer in a fictional context.* I don’t have Eiichiro Oda close by to wrap me with some plot armour, and whatever anxiety that comes with sailing on a massive body of water holds a lot more weight in this physical world that I live in. 

But my swimming classes lately have shown me that it’s not necessarily the water that I’m afraid of. My kicking needs much work, but I now know how to stay afloat, and I don’t have the same paralysing fear of the element that I once did. Water has begun to feel really nice, and on most days, I’m daydreaming of doing cool front-flips underwater. I’m probably also much less anxious than I was growing up, so my mind's ability to anticipate imaginary doom has gone down by several magnitudes. My anxiety and discomfort levels around water have plummeted, but now so has the strength of my (earlier) argument explaining why this rift never did converge.

As I turn 20 this morning, this next bit of word salad is of me searching for something that can make sense of why I’ve (so far) been unable to pull this paradox together. I'm trying to cultivate a custom of posting entries on my birthday that muse on what has passed and think of what is to come. This feels like the correct theme to write on because I’d very much love my 20s to be ridden with adventure, and any realisation (positive or negative) regarding how I exist in an adventurous context will be helpful as I pursue that goal.

### I.

I have never been on a cruise ship, but I would imagine that if you were not ceaselessly filling your time with the incessant entertainment provided aboard, much of the journey would involve gazing into the ocean from the ship you were on. On some days, your eyes might notice the skyline and quickly understand that it was moving as fast, or maybe faster, than you. And that whatever value logged by the captain on the speedometer could never matter, because the horizon always registered itself as uncatchable, and forever quicker than the marvellous bit of human engineering you were calling your vehicle.

I recall being mildly frustrated by this concept for the first time when I listened to Moana’s solo [^1] as a 13-year-old. I know the movie has its fun resolution at the end, but at the time, I couldn’t yet understand why she was longing for something she couldn’t tangibly attain. I knew then that the skyline would always outrun her, and I wondered what exactly was fun about answering the call of a thing that you could never meet. Her purpose, by its nature, was ambiguous, and years later, I can only be impressed by the certainty with which she could walk a path that wasn’t mapped out in bold, black lines.

The first bit of this behaviour that is as impressive as it is difficult is her ability not to put too much weight into the crescendo. Lin-Manuel Miranda’s writing is often very deliberate, and the declaration that she is able to go as far as the horizon in front of her presents her character as indefatigable in a situation that most people would define as tiring. People often sail to get somewhere, and the reason for starting a journey is usually because of where you could be when it is all over: 

- I am going to university because an undergraduate degree gives me much of the credit I need to work seamlessly in today’s society.
- I write code because, [amongst other things](https://onkhida.me/c/across-space-time), I’ve seen and felt the tangible impact that accompanies well-engineered technology.
- As an attacker on the football pitch, the marauding runs I make going forward are not simply for cardiovascular benefits; it is an intentional effort to put myself in positions that enhance my chances of scoring.

*Whether for evolutionary or second-order benefits, every decision is, by definition, a mixture of wants, hopes, dreams, and destinations we want to meet.*

This could be a product of how I’ve developed into adulthood, but there is just much more rigidity surrounding my movements. I’m often thinking deeply about timetables, goals, and scheduling, and much of my present moment is spent planning intricately how I intend to make/meet the OKRs in front of me. But the thing with every goal is that there is always another. Every horizon moves, and no matter how high you reach after hitting the summit of a mountain, there is a descent afterwards and a preparation to ascend the next one. I’ve sometimes felt hungover after this process, and just like I felt at 13 when I realised that there was no finality involved in catching the skyline, chasing objectives that always seem to be in front of me can feel difficult. 

For Luffy and Moana, although they do have larger, culminating goals, the real kick is not necessarily with whatever high that comes during each arc. It feels like they find joy and meaning in the fact that they journey/exist on their respective paths. They sing even when the crescendo has simmered out, and there is no discomfort with the lull and hum that comes with the *ennui* in travel. I think that’s the next step that I need to take. I need to love the journey as much as, or maybe even more than, the checkpoints or success pods that I intend to reach. There is joy in the present. I need to try to stay in it.

### **II.**

I am an above-average chess player. I have not played in forever, but I probably peaked a couple of years back with an Elo around the 1900 mark. Something I recall about playing was that I was much better at solving isolated chess tactics when I was not in a real game scenario. The simple explanation for this could be nerves or time pressure, but I think I was also much better at solving the puzzles because just knowing that there is a puzzle to solve is energising. When solving a puzzle, if I knew that there was, say, a checkmate in three, my mind would reflexively think deeper about the position to find the solution. Getting a hint that the destination is just around the corner is sometimes all you need to take a big step.

And this is the second part about *chasing the skyline* that can feel incredibly difficult: there is a huge amount of belief that is needed to keep going. When a goal feels elusive, every step is like a leap of faith, and if your target can’t be tangibly identified, you just have to hope that the steps you’re taking culminate into something meaningful.  The Straw Hats sailing fearlessly into the Grand Line is seismic because they don’t necessarily know where they are going at the start. They sail boldly because they believe they will get to where they want to be, even though the destination is momentarily obscure. Getting to their destination (even though they do not know where it is) may take 3 days, 3 months, 3 years, or 3 decades, but that doesn’t matter because they are just happy to exist on that path. 

This is difficult because humans are aware that time is a finite resource. Most people don’t want to waste time experimenting and falling into life, and to fight the uncertainty around us, we plan and schedule to a fault. My issue remains that I would love to see every arc, every descent and ascent, in panorama. My brain wants to map out my journey from the start and stay on course until I meet the destination. I’m walking with very little faith, and I’m instead trying to ensure that every step I take is backed by conscientious calculation.

### III.

The odds are that I don’t really have a problem with cruise ships. In fact, it is likely that I might enjoy one if I boarded a ship in real life. My apprehension, at least initially, with the whole experience seems to be tied to my difficulty with just staying in transit for a long time. There is fatigue that comes with floating between destinations, and I have not yet made peace with those interregna.   

My head keeps rejecting the idea of a cruise and the skyline because it makes for a slow and ambiguous activity in a world of speed and solidity. Life is saturated with narratives reminding me to make the most of a cursory existence, and so an activity like that—that just involves me being and not doing—can feel uneasy. There is this article Chimfeka wrote four years ago [^2] that, in some capacity, talked about the difficulty that comes with being human and having sentience. He notes that, unlike us, “the tree never questions itself on how to grow, it just does”, and in many ways, we rob ourselves of the bliss that comes with wafting and dancing to the wind because we are thinking at several degrees higher than common organisms. Maybe if I were a tree, I’d spend days thinking about how to dance to the wind in a way that would be beneficial to me in 5 years. Or perhaps I would spend weeks studying the weather so that I may tilt and grow in a way that would cause the sun to shine favourably on my photosynthetic leaves 10 years from now. 

In essence, the conclusion I’m getting from all of this is that for so long, I’ve been wired to spend so much time thinking about (and doing) the things that have a deeper (and future) benefit to me. I’ve made it difficult for adventure to find me because I keep trying to back up the things I do and the places I am in with some degree of thought. I’m not saying I spend my waking hours as Plato recurrently scrutinising my thought processes, but even the little I do could be providing a buffer big enough to filter out possible adventure and its thrill. My marker today is to try to be more cautious of that. Luck is an important component in any adventure, and although uncertainty is uncomfortable, I need to start embracing it. I’m going to try to find a balance by having more engagements without a clear or definitive destination in sight. I need to make it a practice to enjoy each day of the journey I am on instead of the destination itself. I need to waft and flow to the way the wind blows sometimes, like a tree just happy to exist. I want to feel okay and, in a large sense, be excited about all the things that have not happened yet, because life is uncertain by nature—and no amount of planning can change that.
