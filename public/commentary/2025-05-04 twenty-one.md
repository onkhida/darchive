It is impossible to talk about Lap 21 without talking about my mother. In the preceding years, I’ve had a proxy to help me transform my past experiences into lessons that I may use as artillery to take on the coming year. For 18, I tried [^1] to reflect on my early experiences with chess and its benefits in getting me comfortable with life’s infinite choices; 19 was a reassertion of Vernon’s crux that we are not magnificent, a reminder to feel the gravity of each experience because nothing lasts forever; and 20 was about general lessons on adventure from Monkey D. Luffy and the Straw Hat Pirates. I try to pride myself on the simplicity of each post, and I treat these birthday entries as simple checkpoints that give me an additional opportunity to learn and take stock of the soul within me.

But as I’ve geared up to re-perform this ritual for my 21st, I’ve found a version of me that’s been unable to get in the headspace necessary to conjure anything close to the “birthday post” requirements that I briefly defined in the preceding paragraph. The past 12 months have felt depressing, difficult, and disorienting, unlike anything I have ever experienced. What’s worse is that it has been incessant. It hasn’t just been a steady mellow, and every day has felt like a new, arduous battle with no respite or repose. 

Even in the past periods where I've felt bogged down, I’ve tried to find the light at the end of the tunnel. I have not felt any will to do so in a long time, and any words of hope, encouragement, or positivity have found creative ways of eluding me. And how could I? For I have woken up each day more bemused than the last at the fact that October was a real month on this timeline. It’s been hard to think, write, or even speak coherently, and I feel like I am angry and frustrated at everything. A few days ago, I told a friend that it felt like the sun was mocking me: how could it rise up and shine derisively each day, even when my own sun—my mother—had now flickered out. The months have caused me to lose footing in reality, and as I sit here trying to confront the actuality that today (on my 21st) will be my first birthday without my mother physically with me, I guess it is understandable that I have culminated into a powder keg about to explode.

However, addressing the effervescent, emotional cocktail now buzzing within me is not something I wish to do in this entry. If I set out to explain/explore how deep the current void within me goes, the rivers would run dry before I could finish that task. People I’ve spoken to tell me that grief is an organic process that stews over across time, and I’m trying to handle the wound within with delicate hands. A yarn barf has never responded well to haste, so instead of forcing myself to put words that are destined to fail me on paper, I’ve decided to (at least *try* to) focus on the elements that have given me—albeit in scant quantity—some hope and sanity in the predicament that I’m in.

Hilariously, the beacons I’ve identified all come in the form of music. I listen to a lot of music on a regular [^2], but this already large tally has seen an exponential upturn in the past few. The critique that friends, family, and even parts of myself keep bringing up is that I might be using sound to drown out the melee. I don’t think that is the case. I still sit in silence with my thoughts sometimes, and music has always just been a helpful part of my [sense-making process](https://onkhida.me/c/centre-stage). Hopefully, the paragraphs that follow also support that premise.

### I — A Soft Place To Land

It is an incredible feeling when music you already appreciate reveals itself anew to you further down the line. Even though I’ve never watched the play, I’ve been a fan of the Broadway musical, “Waitress” for forever, speaking ad nauseam in private, and seldom in [public](https://onkhida.me/c/whats-inside), about all the elements that most matter to me.
Of late, the specific song in this catalogue that has interacted most intimately with my being is Jessie Mueller’s ballad, [“A Soft Place To Land”](https://open.spotify.com/intl-fr/track/2diXwZhccjtTaiZN5ZafHZ?si=6ea514ac35a440a3), and it's been the medium that I've run to whenever I've felt like I needed a good, cleansing cry. Listening has been a cathartic experience, and I believe this unburdening is due to how specifically applicable Jenna's [^3] monologue seems to be in my current context.
The first verse of her song establishes Jenna's mother as the "dreamer" who constantly admonished her by saying that "Nothing's impossible, child". My mother, too, dreamt and encouraged me to dream. Even more so, she expressed an unwavering belief in my ability to actualise my dreams into reality. It felt like whatever my hyperactive brain wanted to do was never too outlandish to her, and that I could actually do anything if I genuinely wanted to.
But the particular reason it's made today's list—and as the first entry, mind you—is because it is an important first step. I feel like this song (like my mother did) presents good advice on what we must do when confronted by difficulties. The world is riddled with so much pain and heartbreak, and even though we can only dream intermittently, looking forward to an elusive place of comfort is sometimes the only respite one can have in a desperately desolate world.
It feels like my ["breaking point"](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bBClDyy2uSA&t=1m59s) right now is all that I have, but as a tiny first step, insulating myself by creating a soft place to land.

### II — This Bitter Earth / On The Nature Of Daylight

The last track of Max Richter's 2018 LP, The Blue Notebooks [^4], is sung (posthumously) by the brilliant Dinah Washington. The reason it even appears on this album or forms a composite title with the second track, "On The Nature Of Daylight" [^5], is because he finds a masterful way of incorporating Washington's sample into an already compelling composition. Much like in her original, you can hear her croons and echoes all over the song, but the way Richter cascades over an already powerful track in the background causes both of these songs to coalesce into something much stronger than the mere sum of themselves. 

I can only speak about much of Richter's work from an emotive perspective because I feel as though I lack the relevant knowledge of musical theory or the classics to spin a technical viewpoint together. I can't, unfortunately, tell you where the A minor is on a keyboard, so I can only say what sounds and feels good: this sounds really good!

On Dinah's end, however, she dichotomises her words into two, rather simple, stanzas. The first of these can read as heavily despondent and—in some places—nihilistic. The second stanza seems to follow suit with these themes of dissatisfaction, but right in the middle of it, she inflects and says that "while a voice within me cries, I'm sure someone may answer my call". It is the first glimmer of hope we hear from her, and in the very next line she concludes that it is because of this premise, the possibility that someone may answer her call, that "this bitter earth may not be so bitter after all".

All of this is relevant to me today because it presents a progression that parallels much of how I could navigate this trying time. At the moment, I feel as though I am stuck in Dinah's first stanza: despondent and nihilistic. I can't exactly see it yet, but I'm trying to have the faith that I may progress and inflect as she does in the middle of her second stanza. I'm trying to believe in community and the love of the people around me, that someone will *truly* hear me so that I may also, in turn, proclaim that _"this earth may not be so bitter after all"_.

### III — Nal’ Ithemba (Harbouring Hope)

The tenth track on Msaki’s debut LP, Zaneliza, does more than just name this entry. At 8 minutes and 57 seconds, it stands as by far the longest song on the album. Yet, each time the seeker teeters too close to the brink—about to bring the track to a close—I find my fingers compulsively clicking the “back” button, to restart it all over again. The song has already been on repeat, but I suppose my brain just won’t take any chances. I don’t want it to end.

Its opening piano keys introduce a special kind of warmth that I’ve struggled to find elsewhere, at least in this moment. It is delicate, soft, and encompassing, and well before I begin to hear or internalise Msaki’s words and prayers, the sonics [^6] on this track do well to create an atmosphere that I’d be willing to sit in, even without its powerful lyrics. Had this just been a collection of keys, strings, and the general percussion, it’d be worth it all the same.

But my immense admiration for the general sound and instrumentals does not sink, in any way, my appreciation for the lyrics on this. It is masterfully written, and every line in this song has served a turn in my head as my central motto even beyond this period, a raison d'être. And though it is also very hard to pick a segment that I find most evocative, I find myself particularly enamoured with the [[Verse 2]](https://genius.com/Msaki-nal-ithemba-harbouring-hope-lyrics). She builds a steady crescendo across it, finally grounding us at its climax when she belts: “I′m alive in the promise…made for the deep of the sea.” And it’s what I’ve clutched close to my chest at this point in time. It’s given me a sort of blind faith, a *hope* because I am alive in the promise, “made for the deep of the sea”.

So nal’ ithemba. These have been my signposts. For I know that to become accustomed to this new reality, I will need to dream, so that I may have “a soft place to land”; to hope bravely, so that the “dear wind [may] carry me now, beyond the limits of fear"; and to trust the warmth of my community, so that I may realise that "this earth may not be so bitter after all”.
