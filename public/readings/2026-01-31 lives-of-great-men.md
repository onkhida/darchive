I really do like memoirs. The review behind this one is “Swing Time” [^1] by Zadie Smith, and the review coming soon after this is Kernighan’s “UNIX: A History and a Memoir”. I love stories, and it’s always fun to (at least attempt to) take in the breadth of another’s working human experiences in the pages that they make available to you. Memoirs (or stories written like them) feel intimate: I feel small and naïve once more, like a child hanging on the every word of a close, older relative who is chronicling their adventurous life so far—the achievements, mistakes, lessons, lows, and everything in between. It is for this same reason that I found it difficult to put down Edozien’s memoir once I started it. I have, in fact, read it multiple times before this iteration, but I restarted the process at the turn of the year because I needed its elements to be fresh in my mind whilst writing this review. 

Notwithstanding the *slight* asymmetries between the author’s sexuality and mine, connecting with Edozien was instantaneous because so many aspects of our shared identity overlap. Like him, I am a Nigerian with strong *Igbo* roots that spent a large portion of my formative years in Lagos. We also seemed to (at least initially) grapple with the complexities that come with being a *third culture kid [^2],* and as of late, I’ve toggled between Lagos, my home base; Abuja, where family resides; and Berekuso, in the Eastern Region of Ghana, for school. Prior to this arrangement, I was in South Africa for my A-Levels and looking forward to December visits to Anambra, my mother’s home, for the Christmas holidays. My cumulative travel log does not yet categorise as extensive, but I have (albeit in a measured quantity) seen different places and people, in all their diversity. 

And in this mesh, sexuality has been a pertinent theme. Across the continent, I have witnessed and celebrated queer lives, whilst also grappling (more internally than I should) with the social bigotry towards this demographic in each milieu. And though these fires of hate burn with a different intensity in different spaces, I have seen—on more than one occasion—how vitriolic it gets in many places. *Lives of Great Men* is relevant literature in these times, and I remain grateful for the role Edozien plays as a conduit for social education, awareness, and commentary on the lives that continue to live and love in defiance.

I’m not sure the right move for this review would be to publish all the notes I took whilst reading through each chapter, for I fear I may rob you of the first-hand experience of learning (or re-learning) about the activities within each anecdote. Like most memoirs, it swings around its central axis, the protagonist. However, this book is as much about the scores of men within it as it is about Edozien, and he is successful in his bid to allow each character its own stage and presence, a sense of individuality within the moving prose. I intend to, instead, speak about my favourite of these:

- ***Thomas Morgan, or Tom:*** Upon graduating from college, our writer spends the 3rd chapter, “Out @ Work”, recounting his early work experiences. As a person fresh out of school, it’s livelier than I would have imagined. There’s heavy tragedy, violence, and trauma in this segment and the one’s to come, but with early foundational work at NABJ (National Association of Black Journalists) and then the AFRican Magazine, the positive bit is that he is doing well for himself by climbing up the corporate ladder steadily. As you would expect in the life of someone doing so much in so many new spaces, he is forming relationships in abundance. One of this such was with Thomas Morgan III, a “tall, broad shouldered” man with the “highest-wattage smile” he had ever seen. Although his intentions with “Tom” were initially romantic, they form a platonic bond that sits steadily across the duration of Tom’s life [^3]. His dynamic with Tom sits as the first bullet point I’ve written not just because of its nominal position in the story, but because friendship as a relationship category can be so easily overlooked. There is a different kind of purity in the platonic, and I’m a fan of how Edozien credits him as the reason he “got to understand how gay men could have family outside of blood that ran so deep.” I’m a fan of his relationship with Peter as well, a (then) 51-year-old man he meets in Ghana around 2008, for similar reasons.
